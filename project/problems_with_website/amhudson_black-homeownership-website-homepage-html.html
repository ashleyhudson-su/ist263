Segregation and it's Effect on the Black and African-American Homeownership

De Facto Segregation

De facto segregation is segregation that is not caused by government involvement. These involvements usaally materialize in the form of laws and regulations or government influence. A common argument is that racial isolation is a result of residential patterns and preferences and not a law put in place

However de facto segregation is a myth although people and policy makers use it an an escuse not to address systemic racism in America.

De Jure Segregation

De jure segregation is segregation influenced by federal, state or local govenrmnet action. Specifically, interstate highways were routed to segregate black communities or through black communities which displaced and broke up those neihborhoods.

Segregation and Wealth

Tactics like segregation and the refusal of developers to sell or rent houses to African Americans left African Americans without the ability to accumulate wealth as homeowners and affected their access to jobs or schools. Practices of segregation made living further from white neighborhoods and away from better-funded schools, public services, proper housing, or good jobs. The high-interest rate of subprime mortgage loans that targeted black communities made it difficult to maintain their homes after the housing market crash and therefore bore the brunt of foreclosures.

The data depicts that black and Hispanic people are less liley to recive morgage with low interest rates and are more likely to receive morgage with high interts rates.

For instance, 6% of black people who receive morgage loans pay less than 3% in interest compared to white, hispanic and asian borrowers who receive 8%, 9%, and 12% consecutivley. Additionally, 23% of black people and 18% of Hispanics pay pay more than 6% in interest compared to whites (14%) and Asians (6%).

African-American and Hispanic Homeowners Experience

In 2013, nearly 8% of African Americans and Latinos have lost their homes to foreclosure since 2007.

4.5% of non-Hispanic whites at similar income levels lost their homes

From 1993 to 2000 the number of subprime mortgages in minority neighborhoods rose from 2% to 18%

The foreclosure rate on riskier subprime loans went from 3.3% in 2005 to 15.6% in 2009
